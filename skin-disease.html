Project

Skin Disease Detection & Classification

Computer Vision · Medical Imaging

Overview

This project explores deep learning for dermatology images, building an end-to-end pipeline to classify five common skin disease types. I trained multiple CNN architectures—VGG19, ResNet50, EfficientNet-B0, and DenseNet121—and combined them into an ensemble by averaging softmax outputs to improve stability and robustness.

To make predictions more interpretable, I implemented LAB-based overlays that highlight suspicious lesion regions, giving a simple form of visual explainability on top of the raw probability scores.

Motivation

Early screening of skin diseases can be difficult in resource-constrained settings, where access to dermatologists is limited. While this project is not a clinical tool, the goal was to prototype a decision-support system that can:

  • Recognize a small set of common skin conditions from dermoscopic images.
  • Provide transparent visual cues instead of a black-box label.
  • Serve as a starting point for future work on trustworthy medical imaging models.

Dataset & preprocessing

I used a publicly available skin-lesion dataset (dermoscopic images) and focused on five disease classes. The pipeline included:

  • Resizing and normalization to match the input size of each backbone.
  • Data augmentation (flips, rotations, color jitter) to combat overfitting.
  • Train/validation/test split with patient-level separation where possible.

Modeling approach

For each backbone (VGG19, ResNet50, EfficientNet-B0, DenseNet121), I:

  • Initialized with ImageNet-pretrained weights.
  • Replaced the classification head with a 5-class output layer.
  • Fine-tuned the last several blocks using cross-entropy loss and Adam.

After training, I built an ensemble predictor:

  • Each model outputs class probabilities via softmax.
  • Probabilities are averaged across models.
  • The final prediction is the argmax of the averaged distribution.

Explainability with LAB-based overlays

To make predictions more interpretable, I used the LAB color space to emphasize lesion regions:

  • Converted images to LAB space and analyzed contrast between lesion and background.
  • Generated heatmap-style overlays that highlight high-attention regions.
  • Rendered these overlays as semi-transparent masks on top of the original image.

This provides a simple, architecture-agnostic explanation of where the model is “looking,” complementing the final label.

Results

  • Individual models achieved competitive performance, with test accuracy up to ~74% depending on the architecture.
  • The ensemble reduced variance across runs and improved prediction stability compared to any single model.
  • LAB overlays qualitatively aligned with visible lesion regions in many cases, making outputs easier to sanity-check.

Limitations & next steps

This project is purely research/prototyping and not a medical device. The dataset size is limited, labels may be noisy, and clinical deployment would require larger, curated datasets and collaboration with dermatologists.

Future work could include calibrated probability estimates, stronger uncertainty modeling, and comparisons with more advanced explainability methods such as Grad-CAM.
